## Air Purifier Buying Guide

If you are shopping for the best air purifier for allergies, the decision usually comes down to three practical factors: whether the CADR rating actually matches your room size, how loud the unit is at the fan speed you will realistically use, and how much replacement filters will cost you over a year of ownership.

### Matching CADR and coverage to your actual room size

A purifier's CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) should be at least two-thirds of your room's square footage for effective filtration, and coverage ratings printed on the box often reflect a single air change per hour rather than the four to five changes per hour needed for real allergy relief. For the best air purifier for allergies in large rooms, look specifically for CADR figures rated for multiple air changes per hour, not just the largest square footage number on the packaging.

### Noise level at the speed you will actually use

A purifier rated at 30dB on its lowest setting can climb to 55dB or higher on the speed needed to actually clean a room quickly, and that higher speed is often the one that gets switched off at night because it is too loud to sleep through. Check noise ratings at medium-to-high speeds, not just the lowest setting, since that is the level most allergy sufferers need running consistently.

### Total cost of filter replacement over time

Filters typically need replacement every 6 to 12 months depending on usage and local air quality, and that cost varies significantly between brands using proprietary cartridges versus more widely available generic filters. Factor this ongoing cost into your decision rather than judging purely on the upfront purchase price.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often should you replace an air purifier filter?

Most True HEPA filters need replacement every 6 to 12 months with typical daily use, though homes with pets, smokers, or heavy local pollen and dust will need more frequent changes closer to the 6-month mark. Most units include a filter change indicator light, but if yours does not, a reasonable rule of thumb is to check the filter for visible discoloration every few months and replace it before airflow noticeably weakens.

### What size room can an air purifier actually cover?

The safest approach is to match a purifier's CADR-based coverage rating to your actual room's square footage, then choose a unit rated for a room somewhat larger than yours if you want faster results, since manufacturer coverage numbers are often based on a single air change per hour rather than the more thorough four to five changes per hour that meaningfully reduces allergens. A purifier undersized for its room will run constantly at high speed without ever fully clearing the air.

### Is it safe to run an air purifier overnight?

Yes, running a true HEPA air purifier overnight is generally considered safe and is actually when many allergy sufferers benefit most, since it continuously filters the air in the room where you spend seven or more consecutive hours. The main practical consideration is noise level, which is why models with a dedicated quiet sleep mode, like the Levoit Core 400S or Blueair Blue Pure 511, are worth prioritizing for bedroom use.

### Do air purifiers actually help with pet dander allergies?

A True HEPA filter is rated to capture 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns, which covers the size range of most pet dander, making a properly sized air purifier a genuinely effective tool for reducing airborne pet allergens between regular vacuuming and grooming. It will not eliminate dander that has already settled into carpet or upholstery, so it works best alongside regular cleaning rather than as a total replacement for it.

### How do I clean an air purifier to keep it working effectively?

Most units have a washable pre-filter or exterior grille that should be vacuumed or rinsed every few weeks to prevent large dust buildup from blocking airflow to the main HEPA filter, and manufacturer instructions typically specify how often this outer layer needs attention versus the internal HEPA filter, which is generally replaced rather than washed. Skipping this basic maintenance step is one of the most common reasons a purifier's performance gradually declines even before its filter is officially due for replacement.
