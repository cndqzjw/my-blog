## Pet Carrier Buying Guide

If you are shopping for the best pet carrier, the decision usually comes down to three practical factors: whether you are traveling by air, car, or on foot, your pet's size and temperament under stress, and how much structural protection versus comfort and portability you actually need.

### Sizing the carrier correctly

A carrier should let your pet stand, turn around, and lie down without pressing against every wall, but oversized carriers are exactly what gets flagged as noncompliant for airline under-seat travel, so measure your pet and check the specific airline's published limits rather than trusting a generic "small," "medium," or "large" label. If you are asking what size carrier does my dog need, measure from nose to base of tail and add a small margin, rather than relying on breed averages that vary widely within a single breed.

### Soft-sided versus hard-sided structure

Soft-sided carriers like the Sherpa or Henkelion are lighter, more comfortable to carry, and generally accepted for in-cabin air travel, while hard-sided kennels like the Petmate Vari Kennel are required for cargo-hold travel and offer more rigid protection for rougher handling. Choose based on where the carrier will actually spend most of its time, not just which style looks sturdier on a shelf.

### Matching the carrier to your pet's stress level

An anxious pet generally does better in a carrier with partial visual coverage and secure, multi-point closures, like the Sherpa or Diggs Passenger, rather than a fully open mesh design that can feel exposed under stress. For car travel specifically, prioritize a carrier with an actual safety or crash-test rating over one built only for hand-carrying convenience.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are soft-sided carriers safe for flying?

Soft-sided carriers are generally approved for in-cabin air travel when they meet the specific airline's published under-seat dimensions, but they are not approved for cargo-hold travel, which requires a rigid hard-sided kennel like the Petmate Vari Kennel. Always confirm your exact airline's current carrier policy before booking, since dimension limits and pet fees vary meaningfully between carriers.

### What size carrier does my dog need?

Measure your dog from the tip of the nose to the base of the tail for length, and from the floor to the top of the shoulders for height, then choose a carrier that allows a few extra inches in each dimension for comfortable standing and turning. For air travel specifically, cross-check this measurement against your airline's published maximum dimensions rather than the carrier brand's generic size chart alone.

### How do you get a cat used to a carrier before a vet visit?

Leave the carrier out in a common area with the door open for several days before the trip, placing treats or bedding inside so it stops reading as a sudden, stressful object that only appears right before a vet visit. Cats that associate the carrier with a rushed, anxious moment tend to resist entering it far more than cats who have had time to explore it on their own terms.

### Can you use the same carrier for both cats and dogs?

Many soft-sided carriers work for both small dogs and cats as long as the weight and dimension limits are respected, but temperament matters more than species here, since a nervous cat may do better with more visual coverage than an open-mesh design offers a calmer dog. Hard-sided kennels and slings tend to be more clearly suited to one type of use case, so check the specific product's intended pet type before assuming it works equally well for both.

### Sherpa Original Deluxe vs Diggs Passenger: which is better for a nervous dog?

The Diggs Passenger is the stronger pick for a genuinely anxious dog specifically during car travel, since its crash-test rating and expandable, securely anchored design address both physical safety and anxiety at once. The Sherpa Original Deluxe remains the better all-around choice if your primary need is airline travel or general carrying convenience for a smaller, calmer pet rather than a car-specific anxiety solution.
