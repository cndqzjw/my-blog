## Dog Crate Buying Guide

If you are shopping for the best dog crate, the decision usually comes down to three practical factors: your dog's size and strength relative to the frame, whether anxiety or escape behavior is a factor, and how much you will realistically need to move or fold the crate.

### Sizing the crate to your dog

A crate is correctly sized when your dog can stand up without ducking, turn around fully, and lie down stretched out, with roughly two to four inches of extra space beyond that, not a room's worth of extra floor. If you are searching for the best dog crate for large dogs, measure your dog's height at the shoulder and length nose-to-tail before ordering, since undersized crates are the single most common return reason across every brand on this list.

### Frame strength versus portability

Standard welded-wire crates like the MidWest iCrate are lightweight and affordable but can flex under a strong or anxious dog's repeated pressure, while reinforced options like the Impact Collapsible trade some portability for a frame that resists bending and multi-point latches that resist tampering. Match the frame to your dog's actual behavior history rather than assuming every crate will hold every dog equally well.

### Comfort features for anxious or new dogs

If your dog paces, whines, or refuses to enter a traditional wire crate, prioritize den-style designs with solid or mesh panels over open bar construction, and consider adding a breathable crate cover regardless of which crate you choose. Placement matters as much as the crate itself: a crate in a high-traffic hallway will unsettle an anxious dog far more than the identical crate tucked into a quieter corner.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is crate training cruel?

Crate training is not inherently cruel when done correctly, since dogs are naturally inclined toward den-like spaces and a properly sized, gradually introduced crate functions as a safe retreat rather than a punishment. It becomes a welfare concern only when a crate is used for excessively long stretches, as a substitute for adequate exercise and attention, or introduced through force rather than positive association.

### What size should a dog crate be?

A crate should give your dog just enough room to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably, generally two to four inches beyond their standing height and body length, since a crate that is too large can undermine house training by giving a dog room to eliminate in one corner and sleep in another. Most brands publish size charts by dog weight, but a direct measurement of your specific dog is more reliable than weight alone.

### How long can a dog stay in a crate each day?

Healthy adult dogs can generally tolerate crating for four to six hours at a stretch during the day with adequate exercise before and after, while puppies need much shorter intervals roughly tied to their age in months. A crate should never become a full-day containment solution without meaningful breaks for exercise, water, and elimination.

### Are wire crates or plastic crates better?

Wire crates offer better ventilation and visibility, which most dogs adjust to faster, while plastic or hard-sided crates provide more den-like enclosure and are typically required for airline travel. For everyday home use, wire and mesh-panel designs like those on this list generally suit more dogs than fully enclosed plastic kennels, which can feel confining without adequate ventilation.

The Diggs Revol's diamond-mesh panels and rounded, low-profile shape are specifically built to reduce the visually "caged" feeling that can worsen anxiety, making it the stronger pick for a dog that has struggled to settle in a traditional wire crate.
