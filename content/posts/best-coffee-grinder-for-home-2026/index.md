## Coffee Grinder Buying Guide

If you're comparing burr grinder vs blade grinder for coffee, the short version is that a blade grinder chops beans into random sizes while a burr grinder crushes them to a consistent, adjustable size, and that consistency is what determines whether your coffee tastes balanced or unevenly extracted. Once you've settled on a burr grinder, the real decisions come down to grind range, burr material, and how much daily volume you actually need.

### Grind range versus your brew method

A grinder that only handles drip and French press, like the Fellow Ode, is the wrong choice if espresso is part of your routine, while a wide-range grinder like the Baratza Encore ESP covers both without a second purchase. Match the grinder's stated range to your actual brew methods before comparing price or design.

### Conical versus flat burrs

Conical burrs tend to be more forgiving and produce slightly more fines, which most drip and French press setups tolerate well, while flat burrs like the Fellow Ode's produce a more uniform particle size that pour-over brewing rewards with a cleaner cup. Neither is universally better, the right choice depends on your dominant brew method.

### Daily volume and hopper capacity

A single-cup pour-over habit needs far less hopper capacity than a household running a full drip pot every morning, so match hopper size to your actual bean turnover rather than buying more capacity than you'll use before beans go stale.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is a burr grinder really worth it over a blade grinder?**

Yes, for anyone who cares about consistent flavor, since a blade grinder's uneven particle sizes lead to some grounds over-extracting and turning bitter while larger pieces under-extract and taste sour, all in the same cup. A burr grinder's consistent particle size is one of the highest-impact upgrades available for home coffee, ahead of most brewer upgrades.

**What is the best coffee grinder for pour over and espresso in one unit?**

The Baratza Encore ESP is the strongest single-grinder answer, since its grind range genuinely spans from espresso-fine to drip-coarse without the compromises smaller single-purpose grinders make. A dedicated pour-over grinder like the Fellow Ode will outperform it specifically for pour-over, but only if espresso is not part of your routine.

**How often should I clean my coffee grinder?**

A quick brush-out of the grind chamber every one to two weeks prevents old coffee oils from going rancid and tainting fresh beans, while a deeper cleaning with grinder-cleaning tablets every few months removes buildup a brush alone won't reach. Oily dark-roast beans require more frequent cleaning than lighter roasts.

**Does grind size actually make coffee taste bitter?**

Yes, a grind that's too fine for your brew method over-extracts and tastes bitter, while too coarse a grind under-extracts and tastes sour or weak, which is one of the most common fixable causes of bad-tasting home coffee. **Do I need an expensive grinder if I only drink drip coffee?**

Not necessarily, a mid-range grinder like the Cuisinart Supreme Grind delivers a meaningful consistency upgrade over blade grinding at a fraction of the cost of enthusiast-grade models, and drip brewing is more forgiving of minor grind inconsistency than espresso.
