## Weighted Blanket Buying Guide

If you are shopping for the best weighted blanket for adults, the decision usually comes down to three practical factors: how much the blanket should weigh for your body size, how much heat retention you can tolerate at night, and how much hands-on care you are willing to give it.

### Choosing the right weight

The general guideline recommended by most sleep and occupational therapy sources is 10% of your body weight, plus or minus a pound or two based on personal preference. A 150 lb adult typically does best starting around a 15 lb blanket, while couples sharing a bed often need two individually weighted blankets rather than one blanket sized for the average of two different body weights.

### Managing heat retention

If you run warm at night, prioritize a cooling or moisture-wicking cover fabric, or consider a bead-free knit design like the Bearaby Cotton Napper, over a traditional minky or plush cover that traps more heat. Cotton covers sit in a reasonable middle ground between the coziness of minky and the breathability of a cooling weave.

### Care and cleaning routine

Most glass-bead weighted blankets are not fully machine washable as a complete unit, so check whether a removable duvet cover is included before buying, since that cover is what actually gets washed regularly. If full machine washability matters most to you, a bead-free option like the Bearaby is the more practical long-term choice over a traditional bead-filled blanket.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How heavy should a weighted blanket be for a 150 lb adult?

Following the common 10% of body weight guideline, a 150 lb adult typically starts comfortably around a 15 lb blanket, adjusting up or down by a pound or two based on personal preference for lighter or firmer pressure. It is generally better to size down rather than up if you are between two weight options, since a too-heavy blanket can feel restrictive rather than calming.

### Are weighted blankets actually good for anxiety?

Yes, the deep pressure stimulation from a weighted blanket has been shown in sleep and occupational therapy research to help activate the body's relaxation response, which can ease symptoms of anxiety and support falling asleep faster for many people. Results vary by individual, and a weighted blanket works best as one part of a broader sleep and stress routine rather than a standalone fix.

### Can you machine wash a weighted blanket?

Most glass-bead weighted blankets cannot go through a standard home washing machine as a complete unit and require spot-cleaning or a large-capacity commercial machine, though the removable duvet covers included with most models are fully machine washable. Bead-free knit designs like the Bearaby Cotton Napper are a notable exception, since the entire blanket is machine washable without disassembly.

### Do weighted blankets make you hot at night?

They can, since the added fill material naturally retains more heat than a standard blanket of similar thickness, but choosing a cooling or moisture-wicking cover fabric meaningfully reduces this effect. Hot sleepers who still overheat with cooling covers often do better with a bead-free, open-knit design that prioritizes airflow over a tightly woven cover fabric.

### Weighted blanket vs regular blanket: which is better for sleep?

A regular blanket primarily provides warmth, while a weighted blanket adds deep pressure stimulation on top of warmth, which is the mechanism believed to support relaxation and reduce nighttime restlessness for many sleepers. If warmth alone meets your needs and you have no issues with restlessness or anxiety at bedtime, a standard blanket remains a perfectly reasonable and lower-maintenance choice.
